Visual Displays of data

Tables are a good way of organizing and displaying data. But graphs can be even more helpful in understanding the data. We will start with discussing visual displays of qualitative (categorical) data, followed by visual display of quantitative data.

Qualitative (categorical) data

Two graphs that are used to display qualitative data are pie charts and bar graphs. Both can be easily generated using Technology - either Excel or Google Sheets.

It is a good idea to look at a variety of graphs to see which is the most helpful in displaying the data. We might make different choices of what we think is the “best” graph depending on the data and the context. Our choice also depends on what we are using the data for.

When visualizing quantiative data, we need to pay attention to the context. Using Pie Charts to visualize Quantitative data is only meaningul if the numbers represents parts of the whole. Or, in other words, if the sum is a meaningful number. Let's illustrate it with two examples.

  • Weigh loss over time

Quantitative continous data

Things are a little more tedious when working with continous data. Instead of trying to graph all data, we group it in brackets (often called classes or bins), and then count how many numbers fall in each bracket.

A statistical graph is a tool that helps you learn about the shape or distribution of a sample or a population. A graph can be a more effective way of presenting data than a mass of numbers because we can see where data clusters and where there are only a few data values.
